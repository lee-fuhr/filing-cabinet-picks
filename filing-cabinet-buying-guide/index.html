How to choose the right filing cabinet

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best filing cabinet for you depends on the space you have, the paper size you store, and whether you need mobility. Vertical cabinets save floor space but require more height clearance; lateral cabinets offer more filing width and can double as a credenza. For home offices or tight spaces, a mobile file cabinet (with casters) gives flexibility, but a stationary two-drawer model is sturdier for heavy use. Focus on drawer depth (letter vs legal), lock quality, and the drawer suspension system (full-extension ball-bearing slides are worth the upgrade).

Vertical vs lateral filing cabinet: which layout is right for you?

A vertical filing cabinet is the classic narrow column, typically one drawer wide. Its small footprint makes it easy to tuck into a corner or beside a desk, but the drawers extend far out when opened, so you need clearance in front. Most vertical cabinets come in two- to five-drawer heights. They store files front-to-back. Lateral cabinets are wider (often two or more filing drawer widths) and store files side-to-side, which gives you quick visual access to the entire folder bank. They’re popular in professional offices because they double as a surface for a printer or decor. The trade-off: they take up more wall space and are harder to fit into a tight nook. If you value deep storage and have the floor space, go lateral; if every inch counts, stick with vertical.

Letter vs legal size: why drawer depth matters more than you think

Most home offices use letter-size paper (8.5 by 11 inches), but many filing cabinets are built to legal depth (about 15 inches deep inside). A legal-depth drawer is the safest bet, it comfortably fits letter files plus hanging folders, and it gives you room for those occasional legal documents or bulky dividers. Letter-depth drawers (usually around 14 inches inside) are fine if you never plan to file legal sheets, but they can leave file tabs slightly cramped. When shopping, check the interior clear depth, not just the overall cabinet depth. And remember: a deeper drawer usually means a heavier fully loaded cabinet, so make sure your floor or desk can handle the weight.

Locking files and security: what kind of lock do you really need?

A basic key lock is standard on almost all two-drawer cabinets and is sufficient for keeping casual office visitors or kids away from private papers. Better cabinets use a cylinder lock with a metal core that is harder to pick or bump. For sensitive documents (tax records, client data, legal files), look for a cabinet described as “security” or “commercial grade” with a central locking bar that engages all drawers at once. Keep in mind that no lock is foolproof. A determined thief can pry open most metal cabinet locks, the main purpose is to deter opportunity and satisfy compliance requirements. If you need true high-security, you’ll need a fireproof safe cabinet, not a standard locking file.

Mobile file cabinets: when to roll instead of carry

A mobile file cabinet sits on casters (two locking, two free-rolling) so you can move it between desks or tuck it under a worksurface when not in use. These are excellent for hot-desking environments, shared home offices, or anyone who likes to reconfigure a room often. The trade-off: casters make the cabinet taller (the drawer tops may not line up with a standard desk), and the structure is generally less rigid than a stationary base model. Most mobile two-drawer cabinets are about the height of a standard desk (around 26 to 30 inches) and can roll under a table if the desk has no modesty panel. If you go this route, spend extra on a cabinet with full-extension drawer slides and locking casters, nothing is more frustrating than a heavy drawer that tips the cabinet when opened.

Material and build: metal, wood, or engineered wood?

Steel cabinets are the workhorses of the industry: they resist dents, support heavy loads, and tend to last for decades. The best steel cabinets use 22-gauge or thicker steel for the body and 20-gauge for the drawers. Powder-coated finishes stand up to scratches better than painted ones. Metal cabinets can feel industrial, but many now come in neutral tones (white, black, hammered gray) that blend in. Wood or engineered-wood cabinets (often sold as “home office” lines) look warmer but are less durable under constant daily use. Particleboard can sag over time, especially on wide lateral drawers. If you choose wood, make sure the drawer runners are mounted on steel rails and the bottoms are reinforced. A solid wood veneer or plywood cabinet is heavier and pricier but can last as long as steel if well built.

What size filing cabinet should I buy for a home office?

For a dedicated home office, a two-drawer vertical cabinet (about 15 inches wide) is the most space-efficient if you only need to store one hanging folder per drawer. A two-drawer lateral (30 to 36 inches wide) is better if you have a lot of files and want to use the top surface. Measure your doorways and hallway width first: a full-size lateral cabinet can be awkward to move. If you work from a desk with limited knee clearance, consider a low-height mobile cabinet that fits underneath. Standard desk openings are about 26 to 30 inches tall, but check your desk’s clearance with the casters included. The most common mistake is buying a cabinet that sticks out past the desk or blocks a walkway, always tape out the footprint before you buy.

Frequently asked questions

What is the standard depth of a filing cabinet?

Most vertical filing cabinets have an overall depth of about 27 to 28 inches, with an interior depth of around 24 to 25 inches for letter-size files. Legal-depth cabinets are roughly 30 inches overall and 27 inches inside. Always measure the available space in your room and compare it to the cabinet’s overall depth with the drawer fully open.

How much weight can a filing cabinet hold?

A well-built two-drawer steel cabinet can safely hold between 100 and 150 pounds per drawer when fully loaded with hanging files. Particleboard and budget cabinets typically handle less, around 75 to 100 pounds per drawer. To extend the life of the cabinet, avoid overfilling; a packed drawer is heavy and puts stress on the slides.

Do I really need a locking filing cabinet?

It depends on what you store. If you keep tax returns, medical records, contracts, or any sensitive personal data, a lock is strongly recommended, and in some professions, legally required. For everyday household papers (receipts, manuals, kid artwork), a cabinet without a lock is fine and often cheaper.

Can I use a lateral file cabinet for letter-size files?

Yes, absolutely. Lateral cabinets designed for letter-size paper typically have an interior width of about 30 to 36 inches and use hanging frames that run side-to-side. Just make sure the cabinet is specifically sold for letter size; a “legal” lateral cabinet will work too, but you’ll have a few inches of empty space at each end.

What is the difference between full-extension and partial-extension drawer slides?

Full-extension slides allow the drawer to pull out completely, so you can reach the very back folder without hunching over. Partial-extension slides only come out about three-quarters of the way. For a filing cabinet used frequently or for deep drawers, full-extension slides are well worth the extra cost.

Are two-drawer filing cabinets a good choice for standing desks?

Yes, especially mobile two-drawer cabinets that can roll under an adjustable-height desk. Just check the drawer front height and the desk’s clearance at your lowest standing position. A stationary two-drawer cabinet beside a standing desk also works, but make sure the cabinet top is at a comfortable standing height (around 38 to 42 inches) if you plan to use it as a side surface.

In shortNo single filing cabinet fits every situation, so start with the two biggest variables: the paper size you file most often and the floor space you can spare. A legal-depth steel cabinet with full-extension drawer slides and a solid lock will serve you for years, whether you choose a stationary two-drawer vertical or a rolling lateral. If your budget allows a mid-range model, focus on build quality over extra features, you can always add a label system or divider later.
