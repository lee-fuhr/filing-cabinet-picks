Filing cabinet questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Choosing the right filing cabinet comes down to understanding the trade-offs between vertical and lateral styles, material quality, locking needs, and sizing for your documents and space. This guide answers the most common questions buyers have, helping you make a practical, informed decision without getting lost in jargon.

What types of filing cabinets are available?

The two main categories are vertical and lateral cabinets. Vertical cabinets are narrow and deep, typically around 15 inches wide, and hold folders that face sideways. They save floor space but can tip when more than one drawer is open, so they often come with interlocking mechanisms. Lateral cabinets are wider (30 to 36 inches) and hold folders that face forward, making them easier to rifle through without pulling a drawer all the way out. They are inherently more stable. Mobile file cabinets sit on casters and are designed to roll under a desk or beside a workstation. They usually have one or two drawers and often double as a printer stand or side table. Their smaller footprint makes them ideal for home offices or tight cubicles, but they sacrifice capacity and heavy‑duty construction. A few models combine features, like a two‑drawer vertical cabinet on casters, but most mobile units are lateral in orientation for easier access when parked next to you.

Should I choose a 2-drawer or a taller cabinet?

Two‑drawer cabinets are the most popular choice for home offices and small workspaces because they fit under standard desk surfaces and don’t loom over the room. They force a natural limit on how much paper you accumulate, which can be a hidden benefit for staying organized. The shorter height also makes the top surface usable for a lamp, printer, or decor. Taller three‑ or four‑drawer cabinets store more in the same footprint but can feel imposing, and the top drawer may be too high for comfortable reading when sitting. Weight becomes a concern too: even a fully loaded two‑drawer cabinet is manageable to move, while a four‑drawer unit filled with letter‑sized files can exceed two hundred pounds. If you need serious storage but lack floor space, a tall vertical cabinet is efficient. For most people, though, a two‑drawer model provides the best balance of accessibility and capacity.

How do I know what size filing cabinet to buy?

First, measure your documents. Letter‑size hanging files require a cabinet interior that is 12 inches wide, while legal‑size files need 15 inches. Most cabinet makers list the file size compatibility, but it’s worth double‑checking because some “letter‑size” lateral cabinets actually use a slightly different hanging system. Depth is key: standard letter‑depth cabinets are roughly 25 inches deep, which fits most desk returns. Legal‑depth cabinets add about three inches and may not slide under a desk. Height matters for accessibility. Two‑drawer cabinets typically sit 28 to 30 inches tall, matching most desk heights, so you can reach the top drawer without standing. If you plan to put a monitor or printer on top, make sure the cabinet can support the weight and that the surface is flat and large enough. Always measure the space under your desk or against a wall, leaving an inch of clearance for ventilation and drawer pulls.

What materials are best for a filing cabinet?

Steel is the gold standard for durability and fire resistance. Heavy‑gauge steel (anything over 22‑gauge) resists dents and keeps drawer slides smooth even when fully loaded. Look for full‑extension ball‑bearing slides; they let you see the entire file and prevent sagging. Steel cabinets also come in a wider range of colors and are generally the most affordable long‑term option. Wood cabinets offer a warmer look that blends with traditional or home furniture. However, the drawers often ride on wooden slides or lighter metal tracks that can bind under heavy loads, and the particleboard core may chip or swell in humid rooms. Quality wood cabinets use plywood or solid hardwood, but they cost significantly more. The compromise is a steel body with a wood‑grain laminate finish, which gives you the aesthetic without the structural pitfalls.

Do I need a locking filing cabinet?

If you handle any sensitive documents – tax records, medical files, client contracts, or personal identification – a locking cabinet is a must. Almost all office‑grade cabinets come with a built‑in lock on the top drawer, which engages the other drawers when closed. The standard lock is a simple cam lock, keyed alike so you can use one key for multiple cabinets. For higher security, look for cabinets with a central locking bar or a combination lock. Some models use a push‑button lock that resets with a code. Be aware that many inexpensive “lockable” cabinets only have a push‑to‑lock tab, not a true keyed lock, so inspect the mechanism. Used cabinets often come without keys, but replacements can be ordered from the manufacturer if you have the lock code stamped on the cylinder.

Can I use a mobile file cabinet as a bedside or desk-side table?

Absolutely, and many people do exactly that. A two‑drawer mobile file cabinet is about the same height as a nightstand or side table, and the top surface can hold a lamp, books, or a cup of coffee. The locking casters keep it steady, though you’ll want to lock them if the floor is uneven. The drawer space becomes a hidden catch‑all for chargers, notebooks, or office supplies. The downsides: mobile cabinets are not as sturdy as furniture‑grade end tables, so don’t put a heavy object like a large monitor on top unless the manufacturer lists a weight capacity. The casters also mean the unit may rock slightly when you push on it. For a purely functional side table that also hides clutter, a mobile file cabinet is a smart, budget‑friendly choice.

Frequently asked questions

What is the standard depth of a filing cabinet?

For letter‑size files, the interior depth is usually 25 inches, matching a standard hanging file folder. Legal‑size cabinets add about 3 inches of depth. Always measure from front to back inside the drawer, not the outer cabinet, to confirm your folders will fit.

Are two-drawer filing cabinets stable when the top drawer is open?

Yes, if they are built well. Good two‑drawer cabinets have a wide base and an interlock mechanism that prevents both drawers from opening at once. Without that safety feature, a fully loaded top drawer could tip the cabinet forward, especially on carpet. Look for models that meet the ANSI/BIFMA stability standard.

What does 'lateral' mean in a filing cabinet?

Lateral means the files sit sideways, facing the front of the cabinet, rather than front‑to‑back like in a vertical unit. Lateral cabinets are wider (usually 30 to 36 inches) and allow you to view file tabs without pulling the drawer all the way out, which saves space in tight aisles.

How much weight can a mobile file cabinet hold?

Weight capacity varies widely, but a typical two‑drawer mobile steel cabinet can hold 50 to 75 pounds per drawer when filled with hanging files. Always check the manufacturer’s rating, as cheap particleboard models may warp under far less. Casters add a bit of flexibility but reduce the max load compared to a stationary cabinet.

Can I mount casters on a filing cabinet that doesn't have them?

Yes, if the cabinet has a sturdy metal or wooden base. You’ll need to buy a caster kit with a mounting plate that screws into the bottom. Be sure the casters are rated for the weight of the fully loaded cabinet and that the cabinet’s base is flat and thick enough to hold the screws without stripping.

What is the difference between a vertical and lateral file cabinet?

A vertical cabinet is narrow (roughly 15 inches wide) and deep, with files stored parallel to the drawer sides. A lateral cabinet is wide and shallower, with files stored perpendicular to the drawer front. Lateral cabinets are more stable and easier to browse, but vertical cabinets take up less floor space.

In shortThe best filing cabinet is the one that fits your space, your paper-load, and your peace of mind. Start by measuring your documents and the spot you plan to put it, then decide whether you need lockable drawers and how much you’re willing to spend on materials that last. A well‑chosen steel two‑drawer cabinet with full‑extension slides and a reliable lock will serve you for years without fuss – and that’s really the whole point.
